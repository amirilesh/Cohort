# Cohort Survey – Results & Decision Memo

## Sample size and segments
n = 18 respondents.

English level:
- Native / near-native: 44.4%
- Comfortable but non-native: 38.9%
- Okay but difficult: 16.7%
- Hard / avoid: 0%

So **non-native readers = 55.6%**.

## Key results (numbers)

**Weekly-use intent**
- Mean: **6.5 / 10**
- % scoring 8–10: **38.9%**

**Usefulness**
- Mean: **7.6 / 10**

**Trust in rule-based “study strength” indicator**
- Mean: **6.5 / 10**

**Translation importance**
- Mean: **6.2 / 10**

Observation:
Translation is moderately important overall, but **more relevant for non-native readers (~56% of the sample).**

Additional signals:
- **70.6%** would join a beta waitlist.
- **38.9% search science topics daily**, **22.2% weekly**.
- **83.3% start with Google** when researching a topic.
- **55.6% spend 15–30 minutes** before deciding what information to trust.

## Top pain points (ranked)

1. **Paywalls / cannot access full text — 66.7%**
2. **Hard to tell if a study is strong or reliable — 55.6%**
3. **Hard to find the key takeaway — 33.3%**
4. **Too much jargon / hard to read — 27.8%**
5. **Hard to compare studies — 27.8%**

Interpretation:  
The main problem is **trust and interpretation**, not discovery.

## Feature priorities (ranked)

Features that increase trust:

1. **Evidence-strength rubric with explanation — 66.7%**
2. **Links to related papers — 55.6%**
3. **Clear abstract + DOI link to original paper — 38.9%**
4. **Clear study limitations — 38.9%**
5. **Extracted numbers (sample size, effect size, etc.) — 27.8%**

Interpretation:
Users want **structured evaluation of evidence**, not just summaries.

## Interpretation

The data suggests:

1. People **already search for science frequently**, but mostly through Google or social media.
2. The biggest friction is **understanding whether research is trustworthy**.
3. Users want **structured interpretation of studies**, not just simplified summaries.
4. Access barriers (paywalls) and **difficulty identifying key findings** are major issues.
5. Interest in the concept is strong:
   - usefulness mean **7.6/10**
   - **70.6% beta interest**

Overall, the problem appears **real and validated**, especially among students and people researching health topics.

## Decision

**GO**

The survey indicates:
- strong perceived usefulness
- real pain points
- meaningful beta interest

The concept solves a clear problem around **interpreting scientific evidence**.

## MVP adjustments

Based on the results, the MVP should prioritize:

**Core MVP features**
1. Study cards with:
   - simplified summary
   - key takeaway
   - sample size / study type
2. **Evidence strength indicator** (rule-based scoring)
3. **Clear study limitations**
4. **Direct link to original paper (DOI)**

**Nice-to-have but not MVP**
- translation layer
- advanced comparison tools
- large recommendation graph

**Next experiment**

Build a **clickable prototype + 10–20 study cards** and test:

- Do users understand the evidence score?
- Do they trust the summaries?
- Do they return to use it again?
